TITLE TEXT: Does theta-band power mean a real oscillation?
SUBTITLE TEXT: Welch power alone can lie; only a peak above the 1/f floor is a rhythm

Panel A, top-left: What Welch actually computes
- Welch PSD = average of periodograms over overlapping windows: P(f) = mean_k |FFT(w * x_k)|^2.
- Averaging REDUCES variance of the estimate; it does NOT create structure.
- It gives the distribution of power over frequency, nothing about phase or rhythmicity by itself.

Panel B, top-right: Band power is NOT an oscillation
Draw two spectra side by side, same theta-band integrated power:
(1) pure 1/f aperiodic noise -- sloped line, NO peak;
(2) a real rhythm -- 1/f PLUS a sharp theta bump.
Caption in red: "Integrated theta-band power is high in BOTH. Band power alone cannot tell them apart."

Panel C, bottom-left: The correct test = PROMINENCE
- Fit the 1/f aperiodic floor, then Prominence = peak_power / background(f_peak).
- A rhythm requires a NARROW peak that rises >= 3x above its own 1/f background, not at the band edge.
- Plus: sustained across the run, and phase-locked (nonuniform spike phase, Rayleigh p<0.05; modulation depth).
Our full-scale result: a bump exists but prominence = 1.33x -> band power without a true oscillation.

Panel D, bottom-right: Spikes vs LFP -- two windows on the same rhythm
- Spike-based: population spike-density -> PSD shows the firing-rate rhythm.
- LFP-based: synaptic field -> PSD shows the sub-threshold/synaptic rhythm.
- A genuine theta should appear PROMINENT in BOTH and be phase-consistent between them; agreement guards against a proxy artifact (recall the earlier 68 Hz that was an 8-cell LFP sampling artifact).
